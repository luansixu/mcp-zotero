# Article Structure Templates

## Narrative / Literature Review

### Short (1-2 pages, ~1000 words)

Suitable for: Brief overviews, topic summaries, educational articles.

```
Title
─────
Introduction (150-200 words)
  - Context: What is the topic?
  - Relevance: Why does it matter now?
  - Scope: What will this review cover?

Body Section 1 (250-300 words)
  - Theme-based, not source-based
  - Integrate multiple sources per paragraph
  - Use specific data points from full text

Body Section 2 (250-300 words)
  - Continue thematic organization
  - Address a different aspect of the topic

Conclusions (100-150 words)
  - Summarize key findings
  - Note gaps or future directions
  - Avoid introducing new information

References (8-15 sources)
```

### Medium (4-6 pages, ~3000 words)

Suitable for: Comprehensive reviews, journal submissions.

```
Title

Abstract (150-250 words, structured or unstructured)

Introduction (400-500 words)
  - Broader context
  - Narrowing to specific topic
  - Knowledge gap
  - Objective of this review

Body Section 1 (500-700 words)
Body Section 2 (500-700 words)
Body Section 3 (500-700 words)
[Optional: Body Section 4]

Discussion (300-500 words)
  - Synthesis across sections
  - Strengths and limitations of the evidence
  - Clinical/practical implications
  - Future research directions

Conclusions (100-200 words)

References (25-50 sources)
```

### Long (8+ pages, ~5000+ words)

Suitable for: Invited reviews, comprehensive state-of-the-art.

```
Title

Abstract (structured, 250-350 words)

Introduction (500-800 words)

Background (500-800 words)
  - Historical context
  - Key definitions and concepts

Body Section 1-5 (each 600-1000 words)
  - Organized by theme, chronology, or methodology
  - Each section can have subsections
  - Tables and figures to summarize complex data

Discussion (800-1200 words)
  - Integrate across all body sections
  - Address controversies
  - Identify consensus and disagreement
  - Clinical implications
  - Research priorities

Conclusions (200-300 words)

References (50-100+ sources)
```

## Original Research Article (IMRAD)

```
Title

Abstract (structured: Background, Methods, Results, Conclusions; 250-300 words)

Introduction (500-800 words)
  - Background with citations
  - Knowledge gap
  - Study objective / hypothesis

Methods (variable length, as detailed as needed)
  - Study design
  - Participants (inclusion/exclusion)
  - Intervention / Exposure
  - Outcomes
  - Statistical analysis
  - Ethical approval

Results (variable length)
  - Participant flow
  - Baseline characteristics
  - Primary outcome
  - Secondary outcomes
  - Subgroup analyses
  - Adverse events (if applicable)

Discussion (800-1500 words)
  - Summary of main findings
  - Comparison with existing literature
  - Strengths
  - Limitations
  - Implications

Conclusions (100-200 words)

References (20-40 sources)
```

## Case Report

```
Title (descriptive: "A case of [condition] presenting with [unusual feature]")

Abstract (unstructured, 150-200 words)

Introduction (200-300 words)
  - Why this case is worth reporting
  - Brief background on the condition

Case Presentation (500-1000 words, chronological)
  - Demographics (anonymized)
  - Presenting complaint and history
  - Examination findings
  - Investigations and results
  - Diagnosis
  - Treatment and management
  - Outcome and follow-up

Discussion (500-800 words)
  - Differential diagnosis considered
  - Comparison with similar published cases
  - Pathophysiology or mechanism (if relevant)
  - What this case adds to the literature

Learning Points / Conclusions (100-150 words)
  - 3-5 bullet points summarizing key takeaways

References (10-20 sources)
```

## Systematic Review

```
Title (following PRISMA format)

Abstract (structured per PRISMA: Objective, Data Sources, Study Selection,
          Data Extraction, Results, Conclusions; 300-350 words)

Introduction (500-700 words)
  - Rationale
  - Existing reviews on this topic
  - Objective (PICO format)

Methods (1000-2000 words)
  - Protocol and registration
  - Eligibility criteria
  - Information sources and search strategy
  - Study selection process
  - Data extraction
  - Risk of bias assessment
  - Summary measures
  - Synthesis methods (narrative or meta-analysis)

Results (variable)
  - Study selection (PRISMA flow diagram)
  - Study characteristics (table)
  - Risk of bias (table/figure)
  - Results of individual studies
  - Synthesis of results
  - Additional analyses (sensitivity, subgroup)

Discussion (800-1200 words)
  - Summary of evidence
  - Strengths and limitations
  - Agreement/disagreement with other reviews
  - Implications for practice and research

Conclusions (200-300 words)

References (30-80+ sources)
Supplementary Material (search strategies, full data extraction tables)
```

## General Formatting Notes

### Section Headings
- Use clear, descriptive headings
- Avoid clever or ambiguous titles for scientific articles
- Good: "Clinical Evidence from Phase 3 Trials"
- Bad: "The Proof is in the Pudding"

### Paragraph Structure
- One main idea per paragraph
- Topic sentence first
- Supporting evidence with citations
- Transition to next paragraph

### Tables
Use tables for:
- Comparing study designs across multiple trials
- Summarizing key results
- Listing inclusion/exclusion criteria
- Adverse event profiles

### Language Precision
| Instead of... | Use... |
|---|---|
| "A lot of studies show" | "Multiple randomized trials have demonstrated [refs]" |
| "It was effective" | "Viral suppression was achieved in 93.6% of participants" |
| "Side effects were common" | "Injection site reactions occurred in 86% of participants, of which 99% were grade 1-2" |
| "The drug is safe" | "The safety profile was comparable between groups, with serious adverse events in <5%" |
| "Proves that" | "Provides evidence that" or "Demonstrates that" |
